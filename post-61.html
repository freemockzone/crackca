"The true civilisation is not to be found in the cities and towns but in the villages of India." - Mahatma Gandhi

I. Introduction

  • The Gavri is a 40-day folk ritual celebrated annually by the Bhil community in the Mewar region of Rajasthan, mainly in Udaipur and nearby districts.
  • It is marked by khels (dance-dramas), songs, and ceremonial performances to honour Gorkhiya Mata, an aspect of Parvati, considered the Bhils‚Äô sister.
  • Celebrated after Raksha Bandhan (August full moon).

II. Key Features

1. Community and Belief

  • Bhils trace lineage to Lord Shiva and Goddess Parvati.
  • Festival symbolises their cultural assertion and sacred connection to divine ancestry.

2. Performance Traditions

  • Performed by male-only troupes (due to patriarchal norms), including cross-dressed roles representing women.
  • Performed across villages, especially where their married daughters and sisters live.

3. Themes and Symbolism

  • Subversion of caste and class: Kings, gods, and bureaucrats are parodied.
  • Rabelaisian carnivalesque: Celebratory, humorous, anti-authoritarian, and egalitarian.
  • Reflects tribal resistance, social commentary, and ecological reverence.

III. Social and Cultural Significance

  • Assertion of Bhil identity and sovereignty.
  • Transforms Bhils into temporary deities, breaking social stigmas (e.g., untouchability).
  • Promotes gender expression and fluidity, despite not allowing women to perform.
  • Reaffirms tribal ecological ethics: many dramas warn against violating nature or sovereignty.
