Bihar's Dark Side: Girl Child Trafficking

Date: 2025-07-30 | Subject: Girl Child Trafficking, Human Trafficking, Child Exploitation, Social Issues, Bihar, UPSC CSE | Source: UPSC CSE Notes | Read Time: min

Introduction:

  • Bihar has emerged as a major hub for girl child trafficking in India.
  • The crisis is fueled by poverty, lack of regulatory oversight, and societal acceptance of the commodification of girls.
  • Trafficked girls, often minors, are forced into orchestras, dance troupes, and the flesh trade, enduring severe abuse.

Key Statistics & Scope of the Problem:

  • Bihar Police Rescues (Until June this year): 271 girls (153 into orchestras, 118 into flesh trade).
  • Saran District (Since January): 162 girls rescued from 'dance troupes.'
  • Just Rights for Children (JRC) Partners (March-June): 116 girls rescued from orchestra groups.
  • National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB 2022): 2,878 children trafficked (1,059 girls) - considered a severe underestimation due to underreporting.
  • Global Context (ILO & UNICEF 2024 Estimates): Nearly 138 million children in child labour, with 54 million in hazardous work.

Conditions of Trafficked Girls:

  • Dehumanizing: Stripped of dignity, brutalized into submission.
  • Appalling Living Conditions: Overcrowded, unhygienic rooms.
  • Exploitation: Presented as performers, but subjected to sexual abuse and violence.
  • Vulnerability: Girls as young as 12 sold for as little as ₹10,000.
  • Coercion: Forced to wear inappropriate clothing, dance to vulgar songs for inebriated men. Punishment for refusal, rape for resistance.

Factors Contributing to Bihar's Vulnerability as a Trafficking Destination:

  • Socio-Economic: Deep-rooted poverty in Bihar and source states.
  • Geographical:
    • Porous border with Nepal.
    • Seamless railway connectivity to trafficking-prone states (West Bengal, Jharkhand, Odisha, Chhattisgarh, Assam, Uttar Pradesh).
  • Systemic & Social:
    • Absence of regulatory oversight.
    • Social acceptance of girls being commodified.
    • Exploitation of cultural aspirations (e.g., in West Bengal, desire for daughters to pursue arts).
    • Luring Tactics: False promises of good money, stardom, love, marriage, or employment.
  • "Orchestra Belt" Districts: Saran, Gopalganj, Muzaffarpur, Rohtas, West Champaran are prominent hubs.

Failures in the System:

  • Low Conviction Rates: Despite comprehensive laws, convictions are abysmal. Cases often registered as kidnappings or missing person reports.
  • Under-resourced Anti-Human Trafficking Units (AHTUs): Lack adequate personnel and training.
  • Jurisdictional Issues: Investigations across multiple states collapse due to confusion and bureaucratic delays.
  • Family Complicity/Fear: Many cases go unreported due to family involvement or fear of speaking out.
  • Re-victimization: Rescued girls are often sent back to the same families/environments that facilitated their trafficking.
  • Impunity of Perpetrators: Orchestras continue to operate despite repeated rescues.

Legal Framework (Comprehensive but Ineffectively Implemented):

  • The Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act
  • The Juvenile Justice Act
  • The Protection of Children from Sexual Offences (POCSO) Act
  • The Bonded Labour System (Abolition) Act
  • The Child and Adolescent Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Act
  • Provisions under the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (criminalize child labour, trafficking, sexual exploitation).

Steps Taken & Judicial Intervention:

  • Patna High Court directed Bihar government to act on a petition by Just Rights for Children, recognizing child exploitation in orchestras as a "serious issue."

Recommendations & Way Forward (A Multi-pronged Strategy - PICKET):

1. Prevention (Fundamental):

  • Community Level:
    • Schools: Monitor attendance, trigger alerts for prolonged absence.
    • Panchayats: Maintain migratory registers; mandate action when children disappear.
    • Parental Awareness: Educate parents on trafficking risks.
  • Transport Vigilance:
    • Ramp up Railway Protection Force (RPF) model to inter-State bus routes, local terminals, private carriers.
    • Train transport staff to identify signs of trafficking.

2. Prosecution & Enforcement (Key to Justice & Prevention):

  • AHTUs Strengthening: Full-time, trained officers; cross-border coordination; accountability.
  • Immediate Ban: Absolute prohibition on employment of minors in orchestras.
  • Regulation & Action against Orchestras: Identify, map, regulate; seal premises where girls are confined; prosecute owners/landlords/organizers; attach assets.
  • Labour Department: Mandated to inspect, report, and act.
  • Timely Justice: Time-bound prosecution.
  • Victim Support: Rigorous enforcement of victim compensation schemes.

3. Rehabilitation (Long-term & State-Supervised):

  • Children must not be sent back to exploitative environments.
  • Long-term, comprehensive state-supervised rehabilitation.

4. PICKET Strategy (Holistic Approach):

  • P - Policy: Strong and clear policies prohibiting child labour and exploitation.
  • I - Institutions: Robust institutions for monitoring, prosecuting, and rehabilitating.
  • C - Convergence: Collaboration among agencies, digital infrastructure, and survivor-centered responses.
  • K - Knowledge: Community awareness, intelligence gathering, and leveraging survivor insights.
  • E - Economically: Make trafficking financially unviable for perpetrators.
  • T - Technology: Use technology for tracking traffickers, building databases, generating heat maps, and predicting movement patterns.

Conclusion:

  • Trafficking is a collapse of multiple systems, not just a few.
  • Comprehensive laws exist, but the lack of political will and effective implementation is the major hurdle.
  • Prosecution is a powerful tool for prevention and justice, as evidenced by C-Lab's report.
  • Dismantling the entire system that enables trafficking is the only way to protect future victims.
