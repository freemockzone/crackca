IAF's Fighter Fleet Modernization and Challenges

Source: The Hindu | Publication Date: 2025-08-05

Why in the News?

The Indian Air Force (IAF) is set to retire its last remaining MiG-21 fighter jets in September, marking the end of a six-decade-long service period. This event has brought to the forefront the challenges facing the IAF, including the declining number of fighter squadrons, delays in the induction of indigenously developed aircraft like the LCA-Mk1A, and the broader strategic imperative to modernize the fleet in the face of evolving geopolitical threats. The article highlights the "unending fighter conundrum" of the IAF, focusing on the need to fill the gap created by retiring jets and the slow pace of new inductions.

1. The Retirement of MiG-21s

Service History: Inducted in 1963, the MiG-21s were the IAF's first supersonic and non-Western fighter jets, serving for over six decades. A total of 872 aircraft were inducted, many of them license-manufactured by Hindustan Aeronautics Limited (HAL).

2. Current Status of IAF's Fighter Strength

Squadron Strength: With the retirement of MiG-21s, the fighter squadron strength will drop from 31 to 29, against a sanctioned strength of 42 squadrons. A squadron typically has 16-18 jets.

Inventory Concerns: Besides the MiG-21s, the early variants of Jaguars, Mirage-2000s, and MiG-29s are also nearing retirement by the end of the decade.

Modernization Gap: This creates a significant gap in capability, especially when compared to rivals like China, which has a fleet of around 1,900 fighters, including more than 1,300 fourth-generation aircraft, and has also deployed fifth-generation fighters.

4. Other Key Programs

SU-30MKI: The IAF's most potent Russian-origin fighter, with around 260 jets in service. A major upgrade program for 84 jets has been approved, and a deal for 12 new Sukhois has been signed to replace those lost in crashes.

Engine: AMCA-Mk1 will use the GE-414 engine, while the more advanced AMCA-Mk2 will feature a more powerful 110 KN engine to be co-developed with a foreign partner.

5. Strategic Challenges and Solutions

Filling the Gap: The IAF needs to induct 35-40 jets every year to make up for the shortfall in numbers.

‡§Ö‡§Ç‡§§‡§∞ ‡§ï‡•Ä ‡§™‡•Ç‡§∞‡•ç‡§§‡§ø: ‡§∏‡§Ç‡§ñ‡•ç‡§Ø‡§æ ‡§Æ‡•á‡§Ç ‡§ï‡§Æ‡•Ä ‡§ï‡•Ä ‡§≠‡§∞‡§™‡§æ‡§à ‡§ï‡•á ‡§≤‡§ø‡§è ‡§≠‡§æ‡§∞‡§§‡•Ä‡§Ø ‡§µ‡§æ‡§Ø‡•Å‡§∏‡•á‡§®‡§æ ‡§ï‡•ã ‡§π‡§∞ ‡§∏‡§æ‡§≤ 35-40 ‡§ú‡•á‡§ü ‡§µ‡§ø‡§Æ‡§æ‡§® ‡§∂‡§æ‡§Æ‡§ø‡§≤ ‡§ï‡§∞‡§®‡•á ‡§ï‡•Ä ‡§Ü‡§µ‡§∂‡•ç‡§Ø‡§ï‡§§‡§æ ‡§π‡•à‡•§

Interim Measures: The IAF is exploring the possibility of importing a small number of fifth-generation fighters (e.g., Russian SU-57 or American F-35) as an interim solution while the AMCA is under development.

Future Strategy: The long-term plan is to induct over 600 jets in the next two decades, with a focus on indigenous platforms like the LCA variants and the AMCA, which are central to the IAF's future operational readiness and technological self-reliance.
