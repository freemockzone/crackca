India–US Trade and Foreign Policy under Trump Administration

Published on August 1, 2025

1. Context

  • President Donald Trump has imposed 25% tariffs on India, a developing economy, while offering more lenient terms (15%) to EU and Japan.
  • The move comes after India was optimistic about ongoing negotiations.
  • It coincides with Trump claiming credit for halting Operation Sindoor (India-Pakistan conflict), implicitly demanding acknowledgment from India.

2. Key Issues Identified

a. Trade Policy and Tariffs

  • India labelled as a “tariff king” by Trump; targeted in trade policy.
  • Despite offers like buying more US defence equipment and proposing an FTA, India failed to avoid tariffs.
  • EU and Japan struck deals by committing to investment and economic cooperation.

b. Foreign Policy Challenges

  • Unprecedented alignment of US and China on an issue involving Pakistan.
  • Simultaneous pressure on India to justify ties with Russia—complex diplomatic tightrope.
  • A low point in India’s foreign policy, reflecting a potential strategic miscalculation.

c. Operation Sindoor Ceasefire Link

  • Trump sought credit for peace talks, linking it to trade concessions.
  • Modi government upheld India’s bilateral approach to Pakistan, not crediting Trump.
  • Missed opportunity to acknowledge Trump’s “interest” in peace without compromising diplomatic principles.

3. Analysis of Modi Government's Strategy

a. Misreading Trump’s Personality

  • Failure to manage Trump’s ego, which demanded public credit and gestures.
  • Overestimated Trump’s commitment to India despite:
    • Ignoring Modi during inaugural events.
    • Choosing to engage with Xi Jinping and Pakistan instead.

b. Strategic Miscalculations

  • Many Indian policymakers and diaspora assumed Trump was pro-India, even post-Biden.
  • Pakistan, in contrast, read the shift and invested in Trump’s circle, including deals like oil cooperation.

c. Communication Gap

  • Indian diplomacy appeared unprepared for Trump’s transactional style.
  • Delayed reassessment of US policy direction under Trump.
  • Reliance on symbolic gestures over concrete diplomatic gains.

4. What Could Have Been Done Differently?

  • Modi could have:
    • Publicly acknowledged Trump's "concern for peace", not "credit".
    • Framed ceasefire as a strategic decision by India, with Trump as a helpful observer.
  • Example of alternative statement:
    “We appreciate President Trump’s concern. India remains committed to bilateral resolution once objectives are met.”
  • This would maintain India’s position while satisfying Trump’s need for validation.

5. Broader Implications for Indian Foreign Policy

  • Demonstrates lack of adaptability in foreign policy tools to personalized diplomacy.
  • A cautionary tale on:
    • Over-dependence on individual leaders’ preferences.
    • Need for institutional consistency in foreign policy beyond party leaderships.
  • Urges Parliament to review India’s strategic approach, especially with shifting global alliances.

6. UPSC Mains Relevance

GS Paper II – International Relations

  • India–US Relations: Trade, diplomacy, and strategic engagement.
  • India–Pakistan–China Triangle: Geopolitical positioning and bilateralism.
  • Foreign Policy Tools: Adapting to personalized global leadership styles.

GS Paper III – Economy

  • Impact of Tariffs on trade relations and bilateral agreements.
  • Defence Procurement & Strategic Autonomy in foreign trade decisions.

7. Takeaway for Aspirants

  • International relations are deeply influenced by leader psychology, especially in transactional democracies like the US.
  • Diplomacy must be flexible yet principled—balancing national interest and global realpolitik.
  • Always look beyond symbolism in diplomacy—outcomes matter more than optics.
