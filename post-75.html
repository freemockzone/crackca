Why the Gini Index is wrong about India

What is the Gini Index?

  • A statistical measure of income or wealth inequality.
  • Ranges from 0 (perfect equality) to 100 (perfect inequality).
  • India‚Äôs score of 25.5 places it among the world‚Äôs most equal societies ‚Äî a claim contradicted by multiple forms of inequality on ground.

Criticism of the Gini Index in Indian Context:

  • Large informal employment.
  • Low-income thresholds.
  • Non-reporting of taxable income by most of the population (tax data covers <10% adults).
  • Methodology is data-deficient and blind to structural inequalities.

Forms of Inequality in India:

1. Wealth Inequality:

  • Top 1% earn 22.6% of national income (2022-23).
  • Vast gap between luxury spenders and low-income earners.
  • Most rely on informal employment, lacking social security.

2. Gender Inequality:

  • Female workforce participation: ~35.9%.
  • Leadership roles held by women: only 12.7% (2024).
  • Women-run startups: just 7.5% of total.
  • Patriarchal norms affect education and inheritance.

3. Digital Divide:

  • Only 53.9% schools have Internet access.
  • Rural-urban Internet gap persists.
  • Broadband in homes (urban + rural): 41.8%.
  • Access to virtual education, banking, jobs limited for poor and rural populations.

4. Educational Inequality:

  • Lack of digital tools results in skill gaps.
  • Students without Internet access miss virtual classes (e.g., pollution-related closures in Delhi).
  • Cycle of inequality: Poor digital access ‚Üí low-skill jobs ‚Üí persistent poverty.

Intersectionality of Inequality:

  • Digital + Gender: Only 25% of rural women have Internet access vs. 49% of rural men.
  • Lack of Internet ‚Üí loss of financial, employment, and educational opportunities.
  • Inequality in one realm affects others (health, employment, autonomy).

Conclusion:

  • India‚Äôs Gini Index score does not reflect ground realities.
  • Real equality demands access to opportunities across all domains for the majority.
  • Bridging wealth, gender, digital, and educational divides is essential to become a truly equal society.
