üìç Context

The Supreme Court of India has declined to entertain a petition seeking to bring political parties under the ambit of the POSH Act, 2013 (Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace Act).

‚öñÔ∏è Key Highlights

  • A two-judge bench led by CJI B R Gavai held that the plea falls within the policy domain of the legislature or executive, not the judiciary.
  • The petitioner, Senior Advocate Rekha Gupta, argued for an interpretation of "workplace" and "employee" under the Act to include political parties.
  • The court advised the petitioner to:
    • Challenge the Kerala High Court ruling, which held POSH Act does not apply to political parties.
    • Consider urging women MPs to move a Private Member‚Äôs Bill in Parliament.

üìò About the POSH Act, 2013

  • Aims to prevent and redress sexual harassment at workplaces.
  • Mandates the formation of Internal Complaints Committees (ICCs) at every workplace.
  • Broadly defines:
    • "Workplace": Includes both formal and informal work environments.
    • "Employee": Covers a wide spectrum of workers, including temporary and voluntary.

üö® Issue Raised in Petition

  • Despite broad definitions, political parties often lack ICCs.
  • Women in political engagement (especially grassroots workers) face systemic harassment without institutional redress.
  • Studies cited:
    • UN Women (2013)
    • Inter-Parliamentary Union (2016) ‚Äì Highlight systemic nature of harassment in political spaces.

üßæ Judicial Observations

  • Judicial intervention not appropriate in matters of policy formulation.
  • Legislative intervention (such as a private bill) is the correct route.

üèõÔ∏è Relevance for UPSC

üìå Prelims:

  • POSH Act ‚Äì Features, scope, ICC
  • Separation of powers: Judiciary vs Legislature

üìå Mains:

  • GS II: Governance, Constitution, Judiciary, Women Issues
  • Discuss the scope and limitations of the judiciary in shaping public policy.
  • Analyze the need for extending workplace harassment laws to political organizations.
