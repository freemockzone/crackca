Psychological Impact of Infertility on Women in Delhi

Date: August 1, 2025 | Read Time: 5 min

1. Overview of the Study:

A study titled ‘Infertility and its Psychological Impact’ was conducted by experts from Delhi University (DU) and Lady Hardinge Medical College (LHMC).

The research, commissioned by the National Commission for Women (NCW) in 2019, focused on the mental health of women seeking fertility treatment, specifically looking at stress, anxiety, and depression among women diagnosed with infertility.

The study spanned from 2020 to 2022 and was presented across eight papers, with six already published in peer-reviewed journals.

2. Key Findings:

  • Prevalence of Depression: The study revealed that approximately 77% of women seeking fertility treatment at LHMC were found to be suffering from depression. This figure is starkly higher than the 23% prevalence of depression among women who are not facing infertility issues.
  • Impact of Infertility on Mental Health: The study emphasizes the psychological burden associated with infertility, which often leads to heightened levels of stress, anxiety, and depression in women.

3. Influence of Support Systems:

  • Emotional Support: The study found that women who had spousal support and supportive in-laws or were part of peer groups (such as women facing similar infertility challenges) fared better mentally than those who lacked emotional support.
  • Coping Mechanisms: Women who were able to share strategies for coping with infertility and had a social support system were better able to manage their emotional well-being.

4. Relevance to Delhi’s Demographic Trends:

  • Declining Total Fertility Rate (TFR): Delhi has seen the lowest TFR in India, reflecting an average number of children per woman. This decline in fertility is attributed to several factors, including inflation, increased women in the workforce, better maternal and infant health, independent decision-making, and infertility.
  • Impact of Lifestyle: As women are postponing marriage, childbirth, or opting for fewer children, the stress related to infertility becomes more pronounced.

5. Psychological Support:

  • Need for Psychological Services: The study highlights the importance of integrating psychological support into the fertility treatment process. It suggests that along with medical treatment, there should be mental health services to alleviate the emotional strain of infertility.
  • Peer Support Groups: The study recommends the creation of peer groups where women undergoing infertility treatment can interact and share coping strategies. Such support groups can be instrumental in managing stress and providing emotional comfort.
  • Counseling Services: It is proposed that counseling services be made a part of the infertility treatment process to help women manage the psychological burden and prepare them for the often exhaustive treatment processes.

6. Implications for Public Healthcare System:

  • The study calls for a more holistic approach in the public healthcare system, which should not only provide medical treatment for infertility but also ensure the mental well-being of women.
  • Integration of psychological counseling into the infertility treatment protocol can significantly improve treatment outcomes and reduce mental health problems associated with infertility.

7. Broader Societal and Health Implications:

  • Mental Health in Women’s Health Policy: The findings underscore the importance of considering mental health as a central component of women’s health policy. Infertility-related stress and depression should be recognized as critical factors in women’s reproductive health.
  • Socio-cultural Aspects: In a country like India, where societal expectations around motherhood and fertility are deeply ingrained, the psychological impact of infertility can be exacerbated. This requires a shift in societal attitudes towards greater acceptance and understanding of infertility.
  • Government Initiatives: The study's call for counseling and peer support aligns with the government’s push to strengthen the mental health framework in India, especially in the context of reproductive health.

8. Future Directions and Areas of Concern:

  • Further Research: The study points out the need for further research into the psychological effects of infertility on women, including exploring the impact of socio-economic factors and cultural norms.
  • Policy Recommendations: The findings suggest that there should be policy interventions aimed at reducing stigma surrounding infertility and providing emotional and psychological care as part of standard healthcare services for women.
  • Community-Based Support Systems: Establishing community-based support systems for women facing infertility, especially in urban centers like Delhi, could reduce isolation and provide valuable coping mechanisms.

9. Conclusion:

The study emphasizes the psychological toll of infertility on women and advocates for integrating mental health support into fertility treatment. Addressing the emotional needs of women facing infertility is essential to improving both their mental and physical health outcomes.

As the fertility rate continues to decline, addressing the emotional and psychological aspects of infertility will be crucial in ensuring a balanced and supportive approach to reproductive health in India.
